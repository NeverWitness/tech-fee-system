# Technology Naturally Forms Hierarchies While Knowledge Is Artificially Disseminated

[Previous: The Ubiquity of Technology Fees](../technology-fee/concept.md)

[Back to Index](../index.md)

Previous sections established that:

* Technology fees are pervasive in modern economies
* Technology fees change with technological development and diffusion

To further understand this process, a more fundamental question must be addressed:

**How is technology itself organized and how does it evolve?**

This section proposes two core observations:

* Technology structurally **forms hierarchies naturally**
* The diffusion of knowledge is largely a **human-driven process**

The tension between these two dynamics is essential for understanding both the evolution of technology fees and industrial structures.

---

## I. Technology Naturally Forms Hierarchies

In real production and engineering systems, technology is not flat, but exhibits clear hierarchical structures.
These typically include:

* Foundational technologies
* Intermediate technologies
* Advanced system-level technologies

### 1. Dependency Relationships

Technological hierarchy is first reflected in dependency structures. For example:

* Higher-level technologies depend on lower-level ones
* Advanced systems rely on foundational components

In industrial systems:

* Materials → Processes → Products

In digital systems:

* Hardware → Operating systems → Applications

These dependencies naturally produce hierarchical structures.

---

### 2. Differences in Complexity

Technologies also differ significantly in complexity. For example:

* Basic tools are relatively easy to learn
* Advanced systems require long-term accumulation

As one moves up the hierarchy:

* Complexity increases
* Understanding becomes more difficult
* Implementation costs rise

As a result, higher-level technologies are often concentrated among a small number of actors.

---

### 3. Barriers to Entry

Technological hierarchy is also reflected in barriers to entry.

Lower-level technologies tend to have:

* Low barriers
* Ease of learning
* High diffusion potential

Higher-level technologies typically require:

* Long-term training
* Significant resource investment
* Complex organizational capabilities

This creates a naturally stratified technological system.

---

## II. Hierarchy and Uneven Distribution

Because technology is hierarchical, its distribution is typically uneven. For example:

* Most actors possess foundational technologies
* A small number possess advanced technologies

This pattern can be observed across many fields, including:

* Industrial manufacturing
* Software development
* Scientific research

Thus, technological hierarchy inherently leads to differences in capability.

---

## III. The Necessity of Knowledge Diffusion

Despite its hierarchical structure, the functioning of an economic system depends on **knowledge diffusion**.

If technology does not diffuse:

* Production capacity cannot scale
* Industrial systems cannot form
* Overall efficiency declines

In practice, technological knowledge spreads through various channels, such as:

* Education systems
* Technical documentation
* Engineering standards
* Training and practice

Knowledge diffusion enables more actors to use existing technologies.

---

## IV. The “Artificial” Nature of Knowledge Diffusion

Unlike the natural formation of technological hierarchies, knowledge diffusion is largely a **human-driven process**. Examples include:

* The establishment of education systems
* The creation of technical standards
* Mechanisms for knowledge sharing and openness

These are not outcomes of natural technological evolution, but of institutional and organizational design.
Different institutional environments can lead to very different levels of knowledge diffusion.

---

## V. Technology Diffusion and Technology Fees

Technological hierarchy and knowledge diffusion directly influence technology fees.

In general:

* When a technology is newly developed, few actors possess it, and technology fees are high
* As knowledge diffuses, more users adopt it, and technology fees decline
* Once technology becomes fully widespread, technology fees may approach zero

Thus, changes in technology fees can be understood as:

**The result of the interaction between technological hierarchy and knowledge diffusion.**

---

## VI. Managed Technological Hierarchies

Within the framework of this project, an additional perspective can be proposed:

**Technology can be deliberately structured into manageable hierarchical layers.**

For example:

* Widely diffused technologies
* General technologies
* Advanced critical technologies

In such a system:

* Widely diffused technologies can be accessed with low barriers
* General technologies may require certain conditions
* Advanced technologies may require stricter authorization

This classification does not exactly match natural technological hierarchies, but represents an institutional reconstruction.

---

## VII. Limits of Knowledge Diffusion

Although knowledge can be disseminated, there are still important limitations:

### 1. Cognitive Constraints

Even if technology is publicly available, not everyone can fully understand it. Examples include:

* Advanced engineering techniques
* Complex system design

These still require specialized expertise.

---

### 2. Practical Capability Constraints

Technology is not only knowledge, but also practical capability. For example:

* Process experience
* Engineering debugging skills

These are often difficult to fully transfer through documentation alone.

---

### 3. Organizational Constraints

Complex technologies typically require:

* Team collaboration
* Organizational management

Thus, knowledge diffusion does not imply equal capabilities across actors.

---

## VIII. Summary

Technological systems exhibit two key characteristics:

* Technology naturally forms hierarchical structures
* Knowledge diffusion is largely driven by human institutions

Together, these dynamics shape:

* The distribution of technological capabilities
* The evolution of technology fees

Therefore, understanding technology fees requires considering both:

* Technological hierarchy
* Knowledge diffusion
